Mechanical properties of Dictyostelium Discoideum

  • Date: 2015

This was my M.Phil project, which I did in the Dynamics of Living Matter laboratory, at Cambridge University.

A review of literature concerning the culmination process of Dictyostelium Discoideum showed that a handful of questions oppose researchers. These can be summarized by the following: Mechanically, is the mature fruiting body more than a drop of liquid (the sorus) sticking on a homogeneous cylinder (the stalk) by surface tension?

With a first set of experiments, I showed that a number of qualitative behaviour of the fruiting body such as the stability of the sori suggest that the interactions between the sorus and its stalks are more complex than the model described above.

To quantify the strength of the bond between a sorus and its stalk without affecting the fruiting body structure by touching it, I decided to use its own weight and built an experimental set-up to record the detachment process of Dictyostelim sori under high acceleration peaks, which would affect them like an instant increased gravity.

I collected more data to test two physical models modelling the interaction between the sorus and the stalk. The surface tension model gave poor results, but the friction model had a better fit.

In the end, I concluded that Dictyostelium Discoideum fruiting bodies are not structures as simple as drops of fluid sticking on a homogeneous cylinder by surface tension.

Modeling of an immersed blow-out preventer

  • Date: 2014

This was my project during my internship at the MIT Towing Tank.

The objective of this project was to understand how the different motions imposed by the pipe on the Blowout Preventer (BOP) would affect the generated vorticity pattern and the force applied on the body by the surrounding water, which mimics the way it is likely to happen in the ocean.

I approached this problem with simulations, using the Boundary Data Immerged Body for Cartesian grid method.

I explored different motions (X and Y oscillations, circles, ellipses, 8, butterfly and U motions, both in translation and tilting modes), and used different tools (drag and added mass coefficients, plot of the forces over time, videos and slices of the vorticity, the velocity and the pressure field) to understand how the BOP would be affected. I noticed interesting results with regard to the coefficients and the flow pattern.

Because the geometry of the immerged body was so sophisticated, I was also involved in another project to run simulations of coupled cylinders and observe how they affect each other. This problem was very complex but I could discern a few trends. These results should enable a better understanding of the complex interactions occurring between the cylinders that compose the BOP.

Modeling and comparison of biological adhesive structures

  • Date: 2014

I did this project during my internship at the Cambridge Insect Biomechanics Workgroup.

The objective of this project was to understand how some insect species can control their adhesion when climbing, which involves sticking and detaching their feet countless times without difficulty.

Our hypothesis was that the hairs on their feet were conical and tapered, which would enable them to gradually and progressively control the surface contact, depending on the pressure they impose.

We expected conical hairs to behave very differently and to show a very rough transition in the contact area increment mostly because of the buckling effect, which is so well known by most mechanical engineers.

I built comparative Finite Element Method models that allow me to prove this.

After that I decided to implement the surface tension term in my models, but Abaqus was not able to run these simulations.

Musculoskeletal disorders generated by work postures

  • Date: 2013

I took part in this project during my internship in the Harvard Occupational Biomechanics and Ergonomics laboratory.

We wanted to figure out how to minimise the musculoskeletal disorders generated by a wrong work posture over the years.

To investigate this problem, we first designed entertaining computer tasks using the same movements as office work, and calibrated the sensors. We subsequently recorded the EMG and the position of the different segments of the right arm for several participants during one hour, with different desk organisations (height of the table, sitting or standing...). After that we used matlab to calculate the angles of the angle of the different joints over time, and used PCA on the data collected to figure out which working position generated the least amount of stress and the greatest variability in muscle activity.

Design of a biomimetic ventilation system

  • Date: 2013

This was a group project which I did when I joined the Department of Mechanical Engineering and Materials Sciences at Ecole des Ponts ParisTech.

We got inspired by the pine cone behavior, and used bimetals to design a passive airing system.

The bimetals were laid out in a grid structure, and depending on the temperature they would bend and generates holes, or remain stiff and behave like an hermetic wall.

We used balsa and silicon to build a prototype and compared our experimental results to the ones given by the analytical expression and the finite element method analysis. It appeared that the bimetals were bending more than expected, and in the opposite direction.

This actually ended up being an asset for the design of the grid, but we investigated this surprising phenomenon out of curiosity, and concluded that it was most likely due to the difference in humidity levels that led the balsa to dry.

Ecological impact of hand dryer technologies

  • Date: 2013

This was a group project for a course on innovation and sustainable design at Ecole des Ponts ParisTech.

We used the Life-Cycle Analysis method to compare the ecological footprints of three different hand drying technologies (paper towels, fabric towels and electric hand dryer), keeping in mind the differences in hygien level.

We concluded that the best compromise between our health and the environment were electric hand dryers, if they are modern (airblade technology).

Design and manufacturing of a composite tennis racket

  • Date: 2013

This was a group project during a week dedicated to "Innovation & Design" at Ecole des Ponts ParisTech.

First we designed the racket and made FEA simulations on Abaqus to calculate the Young modulus corresponding to the amplitude of the different deformation modes (stretching, bending, twisting) expected. We also made sure to avoid designing a light, head heavy racket, as those have high-Torque, high-Moment and high-Shock, therefore causing tennis elbow.

We then exported the design on Catia (CAD) to 3D print the mould. We then prepared composites and wrapped them around a tube before oven bake it. Finally we sanded it, drilled holes, installed a grip and braided it.

Solid waste treatment by anaerobic digestion modules

  • Date: 2012

I took part in this project during my internship in the College of Environmental Science and Engineering, at Tongji University (Shanghai).

We optimized the yield of anaerobic digestion modules with an experimental approach: we had several modules with different parameters (ratio of bacterial sludge and solid waste, length of a cycle) and took samples on a regular basis to follow their evolution.

We observed a trade off: the longer we recycle the bacteria, the better the population adapts to the waste and the higher the yield, but at the same time ammonium may accumulate. This toxic compound kills the microbiological flora and decreases the yield.

Conjecture of a limit in sports performance

  • Date: 2012

During our first year at Ecole des Ponts ParisTech. we modeled the human musculoskeletal system in order to conjecture a limit for human sport performance.

After listing all the parameters involved at the cellular and macroscopic level, we used intuitive arguments to confirm that there will indeed be a limit, and explained that it would come from the maximal power that can be generated by a non mutant human musculoskeletal system.

Then, we considered the different trade-offs involved and calculated the orders of magnitude of the parameters describing human muscles (maximal strength vs speed), we decided to apply our model to a specific sport: weight throwing. We made this choice because it allowed us to only consider the anaerobic regime, and to neglect stamina considerations.

Wo modeled the different segments as follow: the bones were considered to be unidimensional rigid beams, connected to each other by zero-moment joints, and the muscles had three components: active, elastic and viscous. They anchored on specific points of the beams, based on anatomical tables. Once the weight is thrown with a given angle and speed, we used a change of coordinate system and simple ballistic equations to calculate its reach.

We then used matlab for the calculations, but could not predict a precise limit because of the inaccuracy of all the biological and anatomical inputs in our model.

Reduction of the noise generated by car tires

  • Date: 2011

I did this project during my last year in preparatory classes.

I investigated the effect of the pattern on car tires, with the aim to reduce noise pollution.

I used a bike and tied pieces of wood in different patterns on his tire. I then put the bike upside down, stroke manually and recorded the noise generated by objects rubbed on it.

After that, I used a software to do a Fourier transformation and the signal and extract its frequency spectrum. The results were intuitive: a regular lay out generates a peak of frequency in the human auditive spectrum (20Hz-20kHz). The more irregular the layout, the more the power is spread over the different frequencies, and the less annoying the noise becomes. A random lay out generates a white noise, which is optimal.

Finally, I confirmed the results by analyzing actual car tires prints.

Testing of an ultrasonic cleaning device

  • Date: 2010

As a project during my second year in preparatory classes, I investigated the efficiency of an ultrasonic cleaner.

First I learned about cavitation to understand how ultrasounds were supposed to help detach a stain from the fabric.

Then I decided to use dye and measure the absorbance of the cleaning water to quantify and compare the efficiency of this technology with classic equivalents, such as hand washing. The cavitation was releasing fabric particules in the solution but I used controls and could demonstrate a slight increase in efficiency compared to hand washing. However, it was still unable to remove most stains.

Humanitarian Aid

As I grow more and more concerned about the increasing inequalities and the challenges they represent, I also get more and more convinced that education is the key.

I am equally worried about global warming* and its consequences on the biodiversity, but I do believe that there will be no solution if the world population keeps increasing exponentially. Therefore I consider that this is an extra reason why the education of the next generation, and more particularly of the girls in poor countries, should be top priority.

I share the utilitarian perspective of Peter Singer and I am supporting two associations, which focus on educating children from poor countries: Aide et Action International and Enfants du Mekong.


*I would like to relay what seems to me an extremely important point: decreasing our red meat consumption is an easy change with huge and positive consequences on both our health and our CO2 footprint.
